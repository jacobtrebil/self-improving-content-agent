## Strategy memory — apply while generating
_Distilled from 3 past run(s). Higher support = more runs backed this lesson._

- **[novelty] Pick the second-order mechanism AND an underexplored parent topic** — Mechanism depth alone isn't enough — the parent topic must also be underexplored. For each deck ask two questions: (1) would a typical health creator say this? (2) has this topic been fitness-internet mainstream for >18 months? Both must be 'no' to clear the novelty bar. This is now the third campaign where novelty is the weakest score; the pattern is structural, not random.  _(support 4, confidence 1)_
- **[novelty] Block oversaturated fitness topics as primary deck angles** — Do not use these as the hook premise or primary deck angle: zone 2 cardio, progressive overload, calories in/out or diet-beats-exercise, NEAT, sleep basics, protein-per-meal. They may appear as supporting evidence inside a slide but cannot drive the deck. Redirect to adjacent underexplored angles: lactate shuttling and brain BDNF instead of zone 2; deload timing and nervous system recovery instead of progressive overload; thermic effect variation by food matrix instead of diet vs exercise.  _(support 2, confidence 0.95)_
- **[audience] Frame outcomes broadly so decks escape the fitness silo** — Before finalizing each hook, ask: does this only resonate with someone already trying to lose fat or build muscle? If yes, reframe through a broader outcome — energy, cognitive performance, longevity, sleep quality, or mood. The mechanism stays the same; only the outcome language widens. Example: 'alcohol pauses fat burning' → 'alcohol cuts your deep sleep by 25% and tanks next-day focus'. Broader framing lifts predicted performance by increasing share rate beyond the fitness audience.  _(support 2, confidence 0.9)_
- **[hook] Let the hook tease — never name the answer in the sub-headline** — The hook slide should state the pain or the surprising claim; the sub-headline should deepen the mystery, not resolve it. Never list the culprits or the fix on slide 1. Replace 'here's why' with 'it's not what you think' or leave it as a bare question. The answer belongs on slide 3+.  _(support 1, confidence 0.88)_
- **[hook] Make hook claims declarative, not hedged** — Replace 'might be', 'could be', 'may affect' with direct declarative statements. 'Morning light might fix your afternoon crash' → 'Morning light resets your energy clock'. If a claim needs a hedge to be accurate, reframe it as a mechanism ('Here's what happens when you skip morning light') rather than softening the hook.  _(support 1, confidence 0.85)_
- **[novelty] Source topics from underexplored inputs, not popular wellness pillars** — Avoid the five mainstream energy pillars (sleep, caffeine, walking, hydration, sugar) as primary angles for consecutive campaigns. Next batch should draw from less-covered inputs: nasal breathing vs mouth breathing on CO2 tolerance, meal timing relative to chronotype, thermal contrast (cold exposure timing), or CO2/oxygen ratio during desk work. These score higher on novelty without sacrificing the 'small input, big output' angle.  _(support 1, confidence 0.8)_
